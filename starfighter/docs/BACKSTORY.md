# Starfighter — Backstory

## TL;DR

It's the late 24th century. You fly a **UEDF Mk-IV Starfighter** defending two
orbital stations from the **Zorgons** — a hive species of insectoid sea-creatures
whose ships flow with a honey-thick fluid they secrete from their own bodies to
stay alive. The catalyst that lets them secrete it came from a pollinator on
their homeworld; that pollinator went extinct. **Earth's bees** still produce
the catalyst — at higher concentrations than anything the Zorgons ever had —
and Earth is the only known source left. The bees themselves cannot live in
Zorgon ships (their atmosphere is lethal to terrestrial insects, like oxygen
to anaerobes), so the Zorgons cannot simply transplant the hives — they need
**ongoing production** from a system whose conditions they cannot occupy.

First contact was peaceful. Scientists from both species cracked the language
gap; humanity, learning the Zorgons faced extinction, agreed to help. Together
the two species **built Aurora Prime** — a wheel city designed to house
joint research and farm the catalyst at scale. It worked. Then the Zorgons
demanded Earth's wild bee supply too, humanity refused, and the cooperation
collapsed. The Zorgons want Aurora Prime **intact**. They are not trying to
destroy it — they are trying to **capture** it, kill the defenders, and
force the survivors to keep producing. Their **Hive-Moon** — a Luna-sized
mobile bio-construct — hangs at the system edge and dispatches the wings
that come for the wheel.

You launch from the **UES Resolute**, a military carrier-station roughly twice
the size of an aircraft carrier, all-military, aircraft-carrier-like in operation.
Nearby orbits **Aurora Prime**, a wheel-shaped civilian city in space — 21,000
souls, full city services, families, schools, hospitals, hydroponic farms,
churches, theaters, holodecks, the works. Together they orbit Earth (or one of
its moons depending on the campaign). A medical frigate (**Mercy**) and fuel
tanker (**Lifeline**) shadow your patrol; if you're in trouble, autopilot
yourself to either one and they will refuel, repair, and rearm you on the fly.
Ship swaps require returning to the Resolute.

Supply convoys from Earth have been cut for months. You and the fleet are all
that stands between humanity and extinction.

There is no final wave. There is only **how long you hold the line.**

---

## Setting — Late 24th Century

Humanity expanded across the solar system over three centuries. Earth remains
home, with the bulk of the species, the seat of government, and the things
worth fighting for. The **United Earth Defense Force (UEDF)** keeps orbital
stations, capital ships, and fighter wings around Earth and its moon, with
forward bases at Mars, the Jovian moons, and a thin scattering of outposts
beyond.

The system was at peace. Then the Zorgons came.

---

## The Threat — The Zorgons

The Zorgons are a hive-organised species of insectoid sea-creatures, evolved on
a world whose oceans were closer to warm honey than seawater. A single Zorgon
worker is the size of a blue whale; their warriors are larger; their queens are
larger still. They breathe a thick, sugar-rich slurry rather than air.

Their ships are built around their biology. The interior of a Zorgon vessel is
flooded with the same honey-like fluid they evolved in — it serves as their
atmosphere, their drinking water, their food store, and their reaction mass. To
a human boarder it would be like trying to fight inside a vat of warm syrup.

The Zorgons **secrete that fluid themselves**, from glands along the dorsal
ridge, the same way a bee secretes wax. It is not a luxury. Without it they
desiccate within hours. Every ship, every hive chamber, every nursery has to
be flooded with fresh secretion at all times — and the secretion only forms
correctly in the presence of one specific catalytic compound their bodies
cannot synthesise.

That compound is the war.

On their homeworld it was produced by a small flying pollinator — their
analogue of a honeybee — and it accumulated naturally in the planet's oceans.
The pollinator went extinct two Zorgon generations ago. Reserves were rationed,
then exhausted. Without the catalyst the secretion thins, the hive chambers
go dry, and Zorgons die in their thousands.

They went looking. Most worlds they surveyed had nothing close. A few had
trace analogues that worked at low efficiency — they stripped those worlds and
moved on; the species living there did not survive the encounter. Then their
scouts found **Earth**, and Earth's **bees**, whose honey contains the
catalyst at concentrations higher than the Zorgons' own extinct pollinator
ever managed. Earth is, as far as Zorgon survey data shows, the **only**
remaining source in this arm of the galaxy.

### Why They Cannot Just Take the Bees

The cruel twist is that **Earth's bees cannot live in a Zorgon environment**.
The honey-fluid atmosphere that keeps a Zorgon alive is lethal to terrestrial
insects within minutes — humid, anoxic, sugar-saturated, the wrong pressure,
the wrong pH. It is the same kind of mismatch that killed off Earth's own
anaerobic life when cyanobacteria began pumping oxygen into the air two
billion years ago: what one species needs to survive is what poisons the
other.

So the Zorgons cannot transplant the hives to their ships and farm them.
They cannot move the production. They need the bees alive, in an oxygen
atmosphere, in Earth-like gravity, in the conditions the bees evolved for —
which means they need either Earth, or a facility that simulates Earth.
And they need it to keep producing, indefinitely, because a single shipment
will not save them. They need a **factory**, not a harvest.

### The Cooperative Period — Why Aurora Prime Exists

First contact was peaceful. Scientists from both species, working in
isolation suits and through chemical-symbol intermediaries, cracked the
language gap. Earth — once it understood the Zorgons faced near-term
species extinction — responded with compassion. The two governments
negotiated a joint program.

**Aurora Prime was the program.** The wheel city was designed and built
*together*, with Zorgon engineering input and human construction crews,
specifically to:

- house joint scientific research into the catalyst and its synthesis
- farm Earth-strain bees at industrial scale in Earth-conditioned habitats
- process honey into the concentrated catalyst extract the Zorgons needed
- ship that extract out to the Hive-Moon on a regular cadence

For roughly eighteen months it worked. Aurora Prime produced. The Zorgons
received. The Hive-Moon held station and did not approach.

Then Zorgon survey data confirmed what their biologists had feared from
the start: Aurora's output, even at full production, would only sustain
their species for another two generations. To survive long-term they would
need access to **Earth's full wild bee population** — every hive on the
planet, redirected, indefinitely. They asked. Earth refused; the wild
population was already in fragile recovery from the 21st-century collapses
and could not bear it.

The Zorgons changed posture. They did not destroy Aurora Prime — they
**need** Aurora Prime — but they began to attack everything *defending* it,
and they began boarding actions designed to take the wheel intact.

### Capture, Not Destruction

This is the doctrine that shapes every fight you will see:

- **Aurora Prime is not a target. It is a prize.** The Zorgons want the
  wheel captured, the production lines running, and the surviving humans
  forced to keep working. Their attacks against the wheel are surgical:
  hull breaches at non-critical points, boarding pods aimed at habitation
  rings, EMP rather than antimatter. They will hurt Aurora. They will not
  finish it.
- **The Resolute, however, *is* a target.** The carrier is the only thing
  that can launch a defending fighter wing. They will gladly destroy her
  outright.
- **Mercy and Lifeline** they treat as soft assets — not prizes, not
  priorities, but worth killing if they happen to be in the way.
- **You** they want dead. A captured Aurora Prime full of cooperating
  scientists is exactly what they need. A live UEDF pilot is not.

That is the war. One species starving and desperate, willing to enslave
another to live. One species refusing to be enslaved, fighting for its
home and its freedom. Both are right. Neither will yield.

Their command vessel — the **Hive-Moon** — is a single mobile bio-construct
roughly the diameter of Luna. It hangs at the system's edge, visible from any
viewport on any clear sightline. It does not approach. It does not need to.
It dispatches drone wings, scout swarms, capital units, and the occasional
queen-ship; what it loses, it grows back.

### Inside the Hive-Moon

The Hive-Moon's interior is not hollow. It is honeycombed — a labyrinth of
hardened honey-secretion that sets into a **Schwarz Diamond gyroid lattice**:
trillions of interlocking triply-periodic minimal-surface chambers, walls of
amber-glass crystal under bioluminescent veins. The passages are wide enough
for a single Mk-IV Starfighter to thread, never wide enough for a capital
ship. Inside live the surviving Zorgon population, the queen's court, the
nurseries, and the mass-production wombs that grow new fighters and bombers
from raw biomass.

One chamber, near the moon's geometric centre, is sized to engulf an O'Neill
wheel station whole. That is what the Hive-Moon is hunting Aurora Prime for —
to ingest her, fold her into the lattice, keep her hydroponic farms and bee
habitats running indefinitely on Zorgon power and Zorgon labour, with the
21,000 humans aboard reduced to maintenance staff for the only catalyst
factory in the galaxy.

If the Resolute falls and the players cannot recover Aurora Prime in time,
the Hive-Moon does exactly that. The wheel is taken. The campaign does not
end — it transforms. The fighter wing must fly *into* the Hive-Moon's
gyroid passages, locate Aurora inside the labyrinth, recover a sample of
the queen's catalyst-bearing honeycomb, and return it to surviving Aurora
scientists who can synthesise a **gyroid solvent** — an isotope-tagged
catalyst that dissolves the Schwarz Diamond walls a little at a time,
opening passages large enough to reach the queen's throne room.

The hive is self-healing. Every passage the solvent opens begins resealing
within minutes. It is a race against time and against waves of sentinels
guarding the inner chambers.

### The Queen

The queen herself is the size of a small capital ship. Her stinger fires
focused plasma; her abdomen and compound eyes are her only vulnerable
surfaces. **The mission is not to kill her.** A dead queen leaves the
Zorgon people leaderless and the war unresolved. The mission is to
**incapacitate** her — strip her shielding, disable her stinger, hold her
in place — long enough to deliver a single message from Aurora Prime's
science council: an antidote has been synthesised that lets Earth bees
thrive in a Zorgon atmosphere. The Zorgons can go home. They can produce
their own catalyst again. They do not have to take Earth.

When the message lands, the queen surrenders. A peace treaty is signed.
The war ends.

The closing cinematic reveals a vast, geometrically impossible vessel
rising at the system's far edge — visibly multidimensional, hostile, and
indifferent to the species distinction between Zorgon and Human. Both will
have to fight together to survive what comes next.

That is the sequel. This game ends with the treaty.

You will never destroy the Hive-Moon. You will fly into it, save Aurora
Prime, and end the war.

## The Two Stations

Humanity's forward defense in this campaign is built on two orbital
installations operating as a paired unit. They orbit Earth, or in some
deployments one of its moons.

### UES Resolute — The Military Station

The Resolute is roughly twice the size of an old wet-navy aircraft carrier.
She is fully mobile under her own power, and she operates very much the way an
aircraft carrier did: launch bays, recovery decks, an air boss, ordnance
elevators, ready rooms, a CIC, an admiral's bridge, and a captain's bridge.

Her crew is entirely military. They live aboard the Resolute when on duty; off
duty most rotate to Aurora Prime to be with families. The Resolute is where
you start the game — your hangar bay, your bunk, your rearm pit, your debrief
room. She is also the only place in the system where you can swap out one
fighter for a different one. Repairs, refuels, and reloads can happen
elsewhere, but a hull change happens at the Resolute or it does not happen.

### Aurora Prime — The Civilian Wheel

Aurora Prime is the city. She is a classic O'Neill-style wheel station with
spin gravity at the rim, and 21,000 people call her home. She was originally
built as the joint human-Zorgon catalyst-research-and-production facility (see
"Cooperative Period" above) — the agricultural rings, the bee habitats, and
the catalyst-extraction labs are still there, still producing, now under
military protection. The city grew up around the factory. She carries
everything a city needs because she has to:

- science laboratories and research wings
- schools from kindergarten through university
- businesses, shopping malls, supermarkets, drug stores
- movie theaters, a full movie studio, holodecks
- churches, sports arenas, concert halls, live theater
- hospitals (men's, women's, children's), clinics (general, mental health,
  women's, men's, children's), social halls
- short-term jail cells (long-term inmates ship back to Earth — when ships
  still ran), courts
- hydroponic farms in stacked layers, water-treatment plants
- nuclear power plants and exterior solar arrays
- museums, recreation areas, observation deck space-view ports, dedicated
  Earth-view ports
- holodecks running Earth-scene simulations for psychological health
- bars, clubs, casino, restaurants, fast food
- police and station security

There is a permanent military presence aboard Aurora Prime for protection,
but they do not police the population. Civilian life is run by civilians.
The military steps in only if a rebellion or insurrection exceeds what station
police can contain.

### Support Vessels — Mercy and Lifeline

Two smaller vessels are always within auto-pilot range of the player:

- **Mercy** — a medical frigate. Auto-dock with her and she will repair hull
  damage and treat the pilot.
- **Lifeline** — a fuel and ordnance tanker. Auto-dock with her and she will
  top off your fuel, recharge your laser cells, and reload your torpedoes.

Neither vessel can replace your ship. If your fighter is too far gone, you
ride back to the Resolute.

## Government and Constitution

Aurora Prime carries the civilian government for both stations. It is a
constitutional civilian republic with a written charter — the Station
Constitution — drafted by the original colonists and amended over the
decades.

- A **President** and **Vice President** lead the executive branch.
- A **Congress**, elected by the station population, represents the people.
- The **Captain and Commander** of Aurora Prime is the highest-ranking officer
  aboard the civilian station, but reports to the President and serves at
  congressional confirmation. Either body can remove a captain deemed
  incompetent, criminal, disloyal, or in violation of the constitution.
- In a declared emergency, the Commander has **absolute tactical authority**
  over the station — but cannot disband or override the political government.
  The civilian government retains the power to remove the Commander even
  during an emergency, on the same grounds as any other removal.

The Station Constitution guarantees a small list of things that everyone on
Aurora Prime gets, no exceptions:

- **Nobody starves.** The hydroponic farms produce enough; rationing is
  population-based, not economic.
- **Everybody works.** If you are physically and mentally able to hold a job,
  you hold one. If you cannot — by injury, illness, or condition — you ship
  back to Earth (or did, before the supply lanes were cut).
- **Everybody is housed.** Billeting is by need, not by rank or wealth.
  Families with children get larger quarters. Singles get smaller ones.
  Nothing aboard a wheel station is large by Earth standards — even the
  family quarters are tighter than a four-star hotel — but nothing is below
  the standard of a decent cruise ship.

Military personnel either bunk with their families on Aurora Prime or live in
barracks aboard the Resolute when on duty rotation. Police and station
security follow the same pattern. Pilots like you usually rotate between the
two depending on patrol schedule.

## The War, Right Now

For the first months of the conflict, supply convoys still ran from Earth and
the Jovian colonies. People could be shipped home. Casualty cases could be
evacuated. New pilots arrived; veteran pilots rotated out.

Six months ago the convoys stopped reaching the station. The Zorgons started
intercepting them at the system edge, near the Hive-Moon. The last three
attempts were destroyed without a single survivor making it back. Earth has
not formally cancelled the supply runs, but no ship has docked at Aurora Prime
or the Resolute in over a hundred days.

The stations are now self-sufficient on food and water. Power is fine — the
reactors and solar arrays carry the load. Ammunition, fuel cells, replacement
fighters, and replacement personnel are not. Every torpedo you spend is one
less torpedo on the rack. Every pilot who ejects and survives is precious;
every one who doesn't, you don't get back.

Earth is the prize. Aurora Prime and the Resolute are the door. You are the
lock.

## Your Ship — UEDF Mk-IV Starfighter

You fly a Mark IV Starfighter — the UEDF's frontline interceptor and the
last clean run of fighters built before the supply lanes closed.

- Single-seat cockpit with full instrument HUD
- Twin laser cannons cross-firing at 180 m for forward concentration
- Proton torpedo launcher with guided tracking
- Turbo afterburner for pursuit and evasion
- Six degrees of freedom — full pitch, yaw, roll
- Ejection seat — never tested by anyone who got to give a recommendation

You launch from the Resolute. You can dock with the Resolute, with Mercy, or
with Lifeline. You cannot dock with Aurora Prime in a fighter — civilian
station, not a flight deck.

## Your Mission — The Sortie Campaign

The campaign is structured as a sequence of **sorties**. Each sortie is a
single deployment from the Resolute with a specific objective. You get
**three lives per sortie**. Lose all three, the sortie fails and you must
retry it before progressing. Complete a sortie successfully and you unlock
the next one — and a new weapon, ability, or hardpoint to take with you.

The full campaign is **seven main sorties**, with sortie 7 itself having
multiple sub-objectives (7a, 7b, 7c). Depending on how aggressively the
player presses the defence of the Resolute, the carrier may survive longer
or fall sooner — the campaign branches accordingly between sorties 4 and 6
to account for that, but always converges on the same final arc inside the
Hive-Moon.

You can re-enter **Training** at any time between sorties. Training is
non-lethal and exists to let you practise movement, gunnery, and any newly
unlocked weapon or ability before risking it in combat.

| # | Name | Objective | Key Unlock |
|---|------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | First Light (Training) | Movement, gunnery, torpedo lock — invulnerable; ends when fuel runs out | Baseline Mk-IV loadout |
| 2 | Picket Line | Hold the patrol perimeter; intercept the first real drone wing | Spread laser |
| 3 | Capital Strike | Kill the first Zorgon dreadnought before it reaches Aurora Prime | Proton torpedo upgrade |
| 4 | Carrier Defence | Keep the Resolute alive through a coordinated assault | Afterburner II |
| 5 | The Long Watch | The Hive-Moon edges closer; bombers and predators in waves | Shield booster |
| 6 | Last Stand | Resolute is lost; Aurora Prime is engulfed by the Hive-Moon | Hardpoint mount |
| 7a | Into the Hive | Enter the Schwarz Diamond gyroid; locate Aurora inside the labyrinth | Lattice navigator |
| 7b | The Honeycomb | Locate and recover the catalyst-bearing honeycomb specimen | Solvent torpedo |
| 7c | The Throne Room | Defeat the sentinels, incapacitate the queen, deliver the message | Peace treaty (campaign end) |

### Sortie Rules

- **Three lives per sortie.** When all three are lost the sortie ends in
  failure and must be retried. No partial credit.
- **No upgrades on failure.** Weapons, abilities, and hardpoints unlock
  only on successful completion of the sortie that gates them.
- **Every sortie has a stated objective.** The objective is shown at
  briefing and tracked on the HUD. The objective ultimately advances the
  arc that ends inside the Hive-Moon.
- **Training is always available.** It does not consume lives, does not
  change campaign state, and is the recommended place to test a newly
  unlocked weapon before the next live sortie.

### Standing Orders for Live Sorties

1. **Launch** from the Resolute's hangar bay.
2. **Engage** Zorgon fighter swarms before they reach Aurora Prime.
3. **Prioritise** Zorgon capital units — they go straight for the stations.
4. **Use Mercy and Lifeline** to stay in the fight; auto-pilot to them when
   shields, fuel, or ordnance run low.
5. **Return to the Resolute** for hull changes or extended downtime.
6. **Hit the sortie objective.** The longer the carrier holds, the more
   sorties you have to work with before the inevitable transition to
   Sortie 6.

## Voices on the Channel

You will hear:

- **The Resolute's CIC** — mission updates, threat calls, vector assignments
- **Aurora Prime PA** — civilian-side announcements, alerts when the station
  takes fire
- **Mercy and Lifeline crews** — refuel and repair confirmations
- **Your wing** — fellow pilots calling kills, calling targets, calling for
  help, calling out the things they're seeing out there

The voices keep you grounded. You are not alone out here. Not yet.
