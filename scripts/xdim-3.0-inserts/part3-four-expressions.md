## §3.5 — The Four Canonical Expressions ("the zoo")

Two operators (gather, explode) crossed with two amplification settings (linear, squared) generate the **four canonical expressions** that the entire visual and acoustic vocabulary of the void-page exhibits is built from. Each has a distinct geometric signature and a distinct perceptual signature; together they are *the zoo*.

| Expression | Family | Geometric signature | Perceptual signature (color · sound) |
|------------|--------|---------------------|--------------------------------------|
| $z = x \cdot y$ | linear gather | smooth saddle; equal trade between x and y; one fair contour per unit step | the calm one — even hue spread, low harmonic spread, smooth contour audible as a sustained tone |
| $z = x \cdot y^2$ | amplified gather | deepening saddle; small y barely moves z, large y dominates fast | gentle middle, screaming edges — hue collapses to band, harmonics flare at boundary |
| $z = x / y$ | linear explode | hyperbolic ridge near $y \to 0$, collapse near $y \to \infty$ | sharp neon ridge near the y-axis, fades as y grows; tone bright at axis, drops to silence outward |
| $z = x / y^2$ | amplified explode | sharp pole at $y \to 0$, very rapid attenuation outward | concentrated spike + wide quiet field — narrow piercing tone with long silent skirt |

**Why exactly these four:**

- **Operator axis** (gather vs. explode) is fundamental — they are the two sides of Ax 2.9 (Operator Semantics). Multiplication unites; division decomposes. There is no third operator at this layer.
- **Amplification axis** (linear vs. squared) is the simplest non-trivial extension that exposes Con 4.3 (Real-Valued Square-Root Constraint) and the asymmetry between solving for `x` (always division) and solving for `y` (square-root when amplified). Higher exponents are scale-equivalent to repeated application of the squared form and add no new geometric character.
- **Together they span** every well-formed substrate in the project: linear-gather is the FastTrack board surface; amplified-gather is the BrickBreaker3D paddle reach curve; linear-explode is the Starfighter weapon-spread field; amplified-explode is the 4DConnect winning-line decay function.

**How to read them as a lens, not a formula:**

Each expression is a **lens** in the sense of Def 1.8 (Manifold Reference Surfaces). It does not *compute* `z` — it *names the bloom surface* on which `z` already lies. To use the lens, you locate the inflection point at $(x, y)$ (per Ax 2.14) and read `z` there. The exhibits on the void page demonstrate this by mapping `z` to a hue and a tone; the user *hears the inflection* as the cursor crosses it.

**Composition rule:**

Substrates compose lenses by **product** (gather) or **quotient** (explode), never by sum. A substrate that needs both a gathering and an exploding character expresses it as $z = (x \cdot y) / r$ with $r \ge 1$ (Resistance Form, §3.3 above) — *not* as a sum of two separate fields. Sum-composition is a category error in this paradigm; it would imply that two manifolds could be addressed at the same observation point, which Ax 2.4 (Observer Primacy) forbids.
