■ The Paradigm ■

What Manifold Is

Manifold is a dimensional programming paradigm — a way of building software where the architecture is a continuous mathematical surface, not a stack of layers.

The governing equation is z = x · y — the hyperbolic paraboloid. Points on that surface can represent real things in the system: a player, a score, a session, a UI element. What those points represent, and how they relate to each other, is the design.

Traditional software has layers: frontend, backend, database, API. Manifold has a surface. Instead of moving data up and down a stack, you move across a geometry. Instead of wiring modules together, you place them at coordinates.

The three surfaces below aren’t decoration. They do something. The math isn’t there to impress — it helps keep behavior consistent and understandable.

■ Design Axioms ■

How It Works in Practice

AXIOM 01
Core systems use coordinates
Players, game events, scores, sessions, and UI states can all be mapped in the same manifold space, so data relationships are easier to reason about.
AXIOM 02
The surface is the architecture
Instead of designing a class hierarchy or a service graph, you place things on a surface. The shape of the surface determines how things relate. Change the surface, change the system.
AXIOM 03
Substrates, not modules
Reusable functionality lives in substrates — dimensional slices of the manifold. Audio, physics, networking, AI each occupy their own substrate. They share one coordinate system, so they compose without plumbing.
AXIOM 04
The rendering is the proof
The gyroid you see in the background, the saddle surface above, the golden ratio in the spacing — the architecture is visible. If you understand what you’re looking at, you can read the system from the screen.
